- Jacob escaped Laban, wrestled with God, and is prepared for the worst from Esau.

**Genesis 33:1-11 > Esau is nice**

- WHAT WAS ESAU’S INTENTION?: kill Jacob and changed his mind, or he thought Jacob might attack him? Esau said “What do you mean by all this company I have met?” Jacob could have replied “What do you mean by these 400 armed men?” How you read this story depends on why you think Esau had 400 armed men with him.
- IMAGINE BEING ONE OF ESAU’S MEN: you have to set out one day, marching for days, every day setting up camp and night, breaking camp in the morning. Finally you catch him, then you hear through the rumor mill that Esau made up with his brother and now we’re headed back home. No fighting to do. Probably seemed like a big waste of time. No spoils of war for you. We never see the lives of the minor players in the story.
- JACOB GIVES ESAU HIS BLESSING: he took it before, and now he is giving some of it back. This is a new side of Jacob. In the reunion of the two brothers, you might think they would be on somewhat equal footing. But Esau does not offer gifts to Jacob like Jacob offered gifts to Esau. **Jacob was definitely the one that needed to make amends.**
- WHAT IS THE FACE OF GOD THING ABOUT? It seems blasphemous. But if anybody knows what the face of God looks like, it is Jacob who was just at Peniel. There is a metaphorical explanation that the night before Jacob was sure he was going to die. The only thing he had that gave him any hope would be the God he wrestled with. So when he actually was delivered from certain death, it might be a little like seeing the face of God. Another explanation is **you held my life in your hands, and I deserved your wrath, and you forgave me.**
- IN THE END: brothers reconcile, Esau is nothing but kind.

**Genesis 33:12-end > Journey’s end**

- JACOB LIES TO ESAU: “if they are driven hard for one day all the flocks will die.” He’s been driving them hard, 30 miles/day, at least until Laban caught up with them.
- DID EASU PREPARE A FEAST? Imagine Esau heading home and preparing a feast for Jacob and Jacob never showed. Next time they see each other is at the funeral. Awkward.

**Genesis 34:1-4 > Rape of Dinah**

- DINAH WANTED SOME FEMALE FRIENDS: 11 brothers can be a lot. But tragedy strikes.
- DINAH VENTURED OUT ALONE? Do you suppose there was some argument that she wanted friends and her brothers and Dad told her it wasn’t safe but she snuck out anyway, thinking they were wrong? The Muslim world blames Dinah in this situation. This is why the beat them if they are caught outside without a male family member and why they have to wear burkas. I blame Shechem.
- COMMON LAW RAPE: rape within your tribe was generally considered wrong in the ancient world, but not as much rape outside your tribe. **The beautiful go to the powerful.** If you had money, you bought any wife you wanted from her father. A good family asked the girl her opinion, but I think rarely did someone with money fail to get the wife they chose. It’s not that big a leap for the rich young man to say well I’ll just sleep with her now while in the throes of passion and take care of the money later.
- NON-VIRGINS WERE OUTCASTS: hard for us to understand, but this boy not only was violent and cruel in that moment, but he ruined her prospects for the rest of her life. She was now an outcast if she doesn’t marry him or if he doesn’t want to marry her. Still this way in the Middle East today.
- THE BOY HAD A CHANGE OF HEART: and decided that he loved her. The beautiful go to the powerful. He clearly didn’t think he had done anything wrong, and he was even trying to prevent her from becoming an outcast. By marrying her rapist.

**Genesis 34:5-12 > Hamor tries to buy Dinah**

- HAMOR IS HEBREW FOR DONKEY: the only humorous spot of this entire sordid affair. Shechem was literally “son of Donkey”
- STANDARD TRANSACTION: Hamor sees this as a standard transaction. He admits no guilt, and Jacob does not appeal to any common sense of moral authority. Clearly, in Hamor’s culture at least, the idea of marrying a girl off to her rapist was unremarkable or at least not worth mentioning in the negotiation. And neither did Jacob bring it up.
- AN OUTRAGEOUS THING IN ISRAEL: at least somebody thinks it’s bad. Interesting that they use the phrasing “in Israel” so early after Jacob getting the name.

**Genesis 34:13-end > Simeon and Levi’s murderous rampage**

- OUR DAUGHTER: Simeon and Levi called their sister Dinah “our daughter”. They did seem to care for their sister as full brothers through Leah. They are negotiating for the family instead of Jacob, like Laban did with Rachel. Jacob never says a word about it except to chastise their murderous rampage.
- NOBODY MENTIONS THE RAPE: did everybody know that everybody knew and it’s the elephant in the room? Did Simeon and Levi’s plan hinge on Hamor and Shechem thinking they didn’t know what Shechem had done to Dinah?
- DID DINAH KNOW THE PLAN?: she goes to live with Shechem. Was he keeping her captive while negotiating? She doesn’t seem to be present at any time except in Shechem’s house when the boys rescue her and murder everyone
- CIRCUMSION IS PLEASING?: this is not how I would describe it…
- MOST RESPECTED: just how “respected” do you have to be to inspire mass circumcision? How much did the town know of Shechem’s rape of and desire for Dinah?
- LIES ALL AROUND: Simeon and Levi lied to Hamor and Shechem, and Halle and Shechem lied to their town about getting all Jacob’s stuff. They promised their stuff to Jacob, not the other way around as they told the people of the town.
- BETRAYAL OF TRUST: Simeon and Levi exploited their deceptive agreement to get into the city without resistance and then kill everybody to rescue Dinah.
- JUSTIFICATION FOR SIMEON AND LEVI: should the rapist have been put to death? There is some chance that Dinah was held captive during the negotiations, and Shechem would not let her go without a wedding. Hamor might even have attacked just to take Dinah for his son if Jacob refused. If so, could they have killed Shechem to rescue Dinah without killing the father and all the other males? Anyone left alive would have pledged vengeance. But when pressed later by Jacob, the boys do not use this as their defense.
- TO THE VICTOR GO THE SPOILS: unlike possible justifications for murder to rescue Dinah, there was no reasonable justification for the looting and the kidnapping done by ALL OF JACOB’S SONS. They basically treated all these women like Shechem treated Dinah.
- JACOB’S SKEWED MORALITY: the only thing Jacob has to say about this whole affair at this time is just a complaint about his own safety. He does not rebuke anyone but Simeon and Levi, and not for murder but for how surrounding nations look at him now. It does, however, persist into their blessing.
- BAD BLESSING: Jacob’s prediction of their lives, he never forgave them for this. **Read Genesis 49:5-7.**
- EYE FOR AN EYE: aren’t you just begging for God to come and say “eye for an eye, tooth for a tooth?” Without God, retribution is “you rape my sister, I murder your entire town.” Modern secular people living in the benefits of Christian culture look on the Old Testament’s “eye for an eye” and self-righteously declare it makes the whole world blind. But without the God who defined perfect justice as you experience the exact pain you inflicted on others, you get the massacre at Shechem. **Every man does what is right in his own eyes. Every man is his own god.**
- GENESIS PREPARES US FOR THE LAW: what everyone wants in these stories is some universal moral code to appeal to. So much bad and pain and awfulness. Broken families, deception, rape, murder. The standard human condition. We don’t always fully appreciate the blessing of the monotheist universal moral code. **By the end of Genesis, we should be begging for the Law to come, begging for the ability to call things definitively right and wrong.**

**Genesis 35:1-4 > remove foreign Gods**

- TURNING POINT: clearly, the wrestling match was a turning point in Jacob‘s life and his relationship with God. You wonder why he did not put away the other gods from his household prior to this.
- GOD COMMANDS AN ALTAR: the only time God tells a patriarch to build an altar.
- DID HE DISCOVER RACHEL’S THEFT? It doesn’t say.

**Genesis 35:5-15 > God’s message, Pillars and altars**

- DEBORAH DIES: here we learn the name of Rebekah’s maid. One of the few women in the Bible whose death is recorded. She must have gone back home at some point after Jacob fled.
- TERROR UPON THE CITIES: Esau may have been acting honestly in offering to send his men with Jacob. If the fear of God had not fallen on the surrounding peoples, it sounds like they would indeed have attacked Jacob. It may have been related to Simeon and Levi’s massacre.
- THERE AND BACK AGAIN INCLUSIO: God’s repeat message to Jacob at the place he gave the original message on the way out of Canaan (Bethel) forms an inclusio around the leaving of Canaan appearance at Bethel and the re-entry to Canaan. **Read Genesis 28:13-15.** Jacob said if you bring me back here you are my God. Jacob removed all foreign god’s and sets up altar in the place he promised that, and now his new name refers to God.

**Genesis 35:15-end > Rachel and Isaac die**

- ROAD BETWEEN BETHEL AND BETHLEHEM: Rachel’s buried beside a random road between Bethel and Bethlehem. Seems so senseless
- JACOB BURIED RACHEL BUT ISRAEL JOURNEYED ON: Jacob’s struggle with God must have been unbearable for him. Rachel was all he ever wanted. **Jacob got right with God at Bethel and the first event after is the death of his favored wife.** I’m sure there were many sleepless nights where Jacob just kept asking God, “Why?”
- PROSPERITY GOSPEL: Do not tie your trust in God to good fortune in your life. It turns God into a celestial butler who “owes” you blessings, and at the first hardship you will fall away from God. But also do not assume God will never bless you, this also is a lack of trust in God. **Trust what God promised you in his word, and trust the love for you he showed through the death of his son to save you.** Practice the faith of Shadrach, Messach, and Abednigo. **Read Daniel 3:13-18.** “Our God is able, but even if he doesn’t...” And the prayer of Jesus in the Garden of Gethsemane: “My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as you will.” Trust that if bad things happen there is a reason, and God will make as many good things happen for you as possible because he cares about you. So keep asking him for blessings. Complain to him about the pain. Talk to him constantly, and honestly, about everything.
- RUEBEN’S BETRAYAL: why? Could be because he wanted Leah his mom to be new queen, not Rachel’s maid. Unclear if Bilhah was complicit. Jacob is not recorded as saying anything, but his blessing in Rueben shows he never forgave him, **Read Genesis 49:3-4.** **Rueben does not get charge of the family as firstborn.**
- ESAU SHOWS FOR FUNERAL: The only thing documented that Esau and Jacob ever did together was bury their father. Esau has all the reason in the world to hate his twin brother Jacob, but he still comes back and buries their father with him.

**Genesis 36:1-end > Esau’s Genealogy**

- EDOM RED: picture of Petra and red rocks, red hair, reminder of the red soup
- UNIVERSAL GOD: an entire chapter of Genesis is devoted to the line of non-Jewish peoples.
- THREAD OF TENSION ALL THE WAY TO JESUS:
  - AMELEK
    - Exodus 17: Attack right after Red Sea.
    - Deuteronomy 25: God is mad, orders future Amalekite destruction.
    - Numbers 14: rogue Israelites enter Canaan without God after spies report, Amalekites beat them.
    - Judges 3: Amalek and Moab oppress Israel 18 years, Judge Ehud stabs fat Moabite King Eglon and locks the door
    - Judges 6: Midianites and Amalekites swarm Israel, Judge Gideon beats them.
    - 1 Samuel 15: God orders Saul to wipe out Amelekites, Saul spares Agag. Tradition says he fathered a child before day before being killed.
    - 1 Samuel 27: David secretly raids Amalekites from Ziklag working for Philistine king Achish
    - 1 Samuel 30: Amalekites burn Ziklag and steal families, David and his men catch and crush them.
    - 1 Chronicles 4: Hezekiah “utterly destroyed” Amalekites
    - Esther 3: the first Hitler, “Haman”, is Agagite, descended from Agag because Saul did not do what God commanded.
  - EDOM
    - Numbers 20: Edom denies Israel passage to promised land.
    - Deuteronomy 2 & 23: Don’t detest an Edomite, they are your brothers.
    - 2 Samuel 8: David defeats Edom and rules it.
    - 1 Kings 11: Hadad the Edomite rebels against Solomon, southern and eastern Edom independent.
    - 2 Kings 8: Western Edom rebels under Jehoram.
    - 2 Kings 14: Amaziah takes Petra temporarily.
    - Obadiah: Entire book is prophecy of Edom’s destruction for gloating over Babylon.
    - Post-Babylon: Babylon chases Edom into Judah, becomes Idumea under Alexander the Great.
    - Maccabees (Hasmoneans) rebel against Seleucid kings after Anitiochus Epiphanies IV and rule for a little while.
    - Hasmonean King/Priest John Hycanus forcibly converts and circumcizes all Idumeans.
    - Pompei conquers Judah for Rome, installs Herod the Great, the Idumean, son of Antipater
    - Herod the Great marries into Hasmonean family for legitimacy: Jewish and Idumean.
    - Herod is called “the great” because he built lots of projects, invented pouring concrete underwater to build Caesarea as man-made deep water port to compete with Tyre.
    - Herod the Great tries to kill Jesus as a baby: murders everyone under 2.
    - There are 7 Herod’s, all Idumean:
      - Herod the Great (37 BC - 4 AD) - murdered babies to try to get Jesus, kingdom splits among 3 sons
      - Herod Archelaus (4 BC - 6 AD)
      - Herod Antipas (4 BC - 39 AD) - beheads John the Baptist
      - Herod Philip (4 BC - 34 AD)
      - Herod Agrippa I (37 AD - 44 AD) - kills James and imprisons Peter
      - Herod Chalcis (41 AD - 48 AD)
      - Herod Agrippa II (50 AD - 93 AD) - Paul preaches to him in Acts 25
    - What started as a sale of birthright over soup culminated in Esau’s descendant trying to kill Jacob’s descendant that was the promise.
